# Sense and Sensibility - The importance of visual suffering

Sense and Sensibility is the most difficult of Jane Austen’s novels to adapt to live action.

The reason for that is the bulk of the drama takes place within the heroine’s head.

Over the course of the narrative, Elinor Dashwood discretely suffers beneath a composed exterior.  (Most of the suffering is her being noble, but part of it is her character arc where she realizes she has bottled up too much pain and could have relied more on others.  Just because some key characters are unreliable doesn’t mean *all* the characters are.)

Live action is primarily a visual medium.

Audio is important, but the general rule of thumb is to convey most information visually, and use audio for information that cannot be practically conveyed visually.

Unless a live action adaptation of Sense and Sensibility relied on voice over or some kind of abstract dream sequences (both of which would be lame) , there’s no practical way to convey what Elinor is really going through.

Because of that, every Sense and Sensibility adaption has focused on secondary aspects of the story and downplayed the greatest dramatic thread of the novel.
