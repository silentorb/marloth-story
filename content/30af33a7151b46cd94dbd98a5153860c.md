# Superstition and fantasy

Superstition is pride.  Instead of humbly saying, “I don’t know what is causing this phenomena,” people make wild guesses and treat them as facts.

I would like to incorporate more [Folklore](../../Features/Folklore%20b9b9e0b8df614e64bae9bf2ec5b048a2.md) into Marloth, but a large part of what interests me about folklore is superstition.

I could debunk superstition in the narrative, but the main aspect of superstition which interests me is its fantasy.  Within the narrative setting, I would want the superstition to be real.

A possible solution would be to dissect folklore superstition and see if there are neutral qualities I can extract from it, leaving behind the dross.

# Superstition and religion

Many and possibly all of the problems with superstition stem from pagan religion.

> The fear of the Lord is the beginning of wisdom.

When people reject God and turn to idols, they are left to their own imaginations.

Moral, neutral, and immoral practices

Some superstition promotes virtue (though it is a fragile promotion.)

Some superstition is neutral—a mostly harmless novelty.

Some superstition is immoral.

I am least interested in neutral practices.  I am more interested in practices with moral consequences.
