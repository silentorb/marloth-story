# Overview

[[01KWN86X6NJZMP5ZESZTNDXXXZ]] has become one of the primary influences on my art over the past 15 years.

It's influence on my game design is fairly straightforward, considering that Dark Souls is a game.

However, surprisingly it is one of the primary inspirations on my story writing as well.

While I have extensive cross-links between [[01KWN86X6NJZMP5ZESZTNDXXXZ]] and [[01KWN86X6NJZMP5ZESZTNDXY7W]], the influence of this source is profound enough to warrant a more granular analysis of what fascinates me about [[01KWN86X6NJZMP5ZESZTNDXXXZ]].

Part of my interest in this exercise is to see if I can extract some of the more elusive and sublime ideas I want to port to my storytelling.

# The series

Throughout this analysis I will be considering all three games in the [[01KWN86X6NJZMP5ZESZTNDXXXZ]] series.

However, in a pinch, the first game does contain all of the ideas I am mining the series for and is sufficient for analysis.

However, the second and third games do add further examples and provide a more comprehensive data sample pool to reference.

# The world

By far, my favorite feature of [[01KWN86X6NJZMP5ZESZTNDXXXZ]] is its world.

At a high-level, its world is:

1. 3D
2. Organic
3. Strongly vertical
4. Mostly contiguous
5. Open-ended
6. Looping

# Solo adventure

While I am trying to unify [[01KWN86X6MFZQAJ1V36T9592ES]], I do relate more to adventure than society.

I feel at home wandering around the wilderness on my own, fighting monsters, and occasionally talking to other characters.

That's similar to my Silent Orb aesthetic of dark background with splashes of light, I suppose I do prefer a solo adventure with splashes of socialization more than socialization with splashes of solo adventure.

# Contiguous world

Except for a few teleporting exceptions, the worlds of the three games are all contiguous.

I am sensitive to continuity in general so I think this kind of layout resonates with me.

# Character location dissonance

## Near combat

The Souls games will occasionally feature locations where a monster that is trying to kill you is right next to a character you can talk to.

It's very [[01KWN86X6NJZMP5ZESZTNDXY71]] to be fighting for your life and hear a merchant or blacksmith muttering to themselves as they work nearby.

## Odd location

Sometimes the location isn't right next to combat but an odd location.

Fighting through a tower and finding the laddersmith casually standing outside on a ledge.

## Characters with fuzzy memory

This is specific to Dark Souls 2.

In Dark Souls 2, a common pattern between the friendly NPCs you meet is amnesia as to:

1. How they arrived at the land
2. What their goals are (they usually have a minimal notion that returns to them as they are conversing with you

# Notable highlights in the series

## Starting in the Undead Asylum - DS1

After a cinematic providing lots of background I'm not particularly interested in, the player finds himself in a prison cell in the Undead Asylum.

The player is a weak, pathetic undead, wearing nothing but rags.

A trap door slides open in the ceiling and a body is dropped into the cell.

A helmeted head looks down at the player from outside before closing the trap door.

The body has a key which allows you to unlock your cell and escape.

At the time it is unclear whether the person above was friend or foe—whether they dropped you the body with the intent of giving you the key, or if the key was a happy accident.

Either way, the whole event feels providential.

A shriveled undead is shown grace so that he can set out on a grand quest and grow strong, changing both himself and the world.

> 💡Later on it turns out that the helmeted figure was Oscar, Knight of Astoria, and it was his intent to help you.

> 💡The theme of nobodies doing great things is very Tolkienesque, though part of the intentional irony of the Dark Souls series is the storylines is ultimately absurd and the grandeur is an illusion.

## Oscar, Knight of Astoria - DS2

1. You first see a glimpse of Oscar through an opening in the wall but can't access the room he's in yet.
2. You can't immediately access the room he's in from any angle.  Proceeding past it causes an undead to push a boulder down a flight of stairs and break a hole through the wall into the room Oscar is in.
3. Oscar is lying in debris, dying.

I can't fully put my finger on why, but every time I reach this point it just feels so epic.

Building on the themes noted earlier with the start of the Undead Asylum, it evokes a sense of humble characters doing great things.

Also, Oscar's brief story is a mix of tragedy and heroism.

I think part of what impresses me is how all of that is bundled into a minute of gameplay.

## Cromwell - DS2

Cromwell is a priest in Dark Souls 2.

> *Something seems to be bothering you*
>
> *Yes, yes, I can tell. It is written upon your face. You have doubts about something you did.*
>
> *I am Cromwell.*
>
> *Regret, anguish, disillusion, bewilderment. What wonderful gifts they are. Together, they are the essence of life.*
>
> *Don't you agree?*
>
> *But now, you are lost, bewildered by your own actions. You poor little thing.*
>
> *Yes, yes, how tiny and frail are we.*
>
> *But if your heart is yet sincere, your sins will be forgiven. Now is the chance.*
>
> *Demonstrate your sincerity to me.*

His dialog is so insidious and ironic.

What's also ironic is is location.

After fighting your way through catacombs full of giant spiders, the catacombs lead to a lightweight boss fight in a small church building.

The boss fight involves an evil priest with an undead congregation.

After the fight you can exit the church.  Outside the church is a ladder to an attic, where Cromwell is hanging out.

The attic is full of giant spiders and the corpses of their victims.

The spiders in the attic do not attack you.

There's an eerie sense that Cromwell is friends with the monstrous spiders you've been fighting.

That further adds suspicion that he may not have been entirely opposed to the corrupt forces you defeated in the church below.

The fact that spiders *aren't* attacking you anymore also looks bad for the player.

It's as though villainy is no longer bothering you because somehow you are now complicit with that villainy.

## Rescuing Karla - DS3

At one point in the Irithyll Dungeon, the player can free the witch Karla from prison.

Like many NPCs in the game, she will then relocate to your home base: the Firelink Shrine.

This event is particularly striking and effective to me because:

1. It's unexpected, yet makes sense.  It took me multiple playthroughs before I remembered ahead of time that she was behind that cell door.  (Note that this is after exploring many other cells in the dungeon.)
2. She is tucked away in a particularly troublesome area that has many jailers and takes a lot of patience to get through.
3. You can't immediately free her.  You have to get through that area once, continue on through further dungeons that loop back around, and find the key to her cell along the way.
4. She initially mistakes you for one of her jailers.
5. She is very melancholy and sympathetic
6. While dungeons and imprisoned characters to be freed are a common pattern in the series, this is the only case where the jailors are so prominent (and annoying).  It feels more grounded with a sense-of-place.
7. She has that INFP mix of cute innocence and fascination with darkness
8. She returns to your home base, making it feel like you just found another friend to join your [[01KWN86X6NJZMP5ZESZTNDXY2R]].
9. If you're building a dark magic mage, she is the primary character to equip you for that.  (This might seem irrelevant but the point is al of this isn't just style, it effects the gameplay in significant ways)

Whatever the causes, every time I free Karla I find it inspiring and it makes me want to write Marloth stories and somehow tap into whatever it is that just happened.

## Dancer arrival - DS3

In the High Wall of Lothric (a city) there is a gloomy cathedral with a large, open room.

At the far end of the room is a throne-like chair upon which sits an elderly woman.

At one point of the game, the woman dies, triggering a cutscene.

As the woman is dying, she warns you about what you need to do next and what is coming.

The camera turns to face the double door way behind you.

The atmosphere grows dark, wind begins to blow, candles are snuffed out, and the doors magically slam shut.

Then black, supernatural goo begins to drip from the ceiling.

The camera tilts up, revealing a horrific figure oozing out of the thin air.  (It sort of looks like the apparition is arriving through a portal, but the visual effects are blurred to preserve some ambiguity as to what is happening.)

The massive figure drops down to the floor, wielding two equally massive swords.

The figure is the Dancer of the Boreal Valley, and thus begins the next boss fight.

I find this scene striking because:

1. It is unexpected, yet makes sense.
2. It draws attention to set pieces that the player would normally take for granted (effectively encouraging the player to "smell the flowers").
3. It is a good mix of ordinary and extraordinary—it feels supernatural in a creative way, particularly the way the door closes on its own..
4. The way the dancer enters the world is complex and a work of art in itself.
5. There is a mystery as to where the dancer is coming from.  Is it teleporting from a nearby location?  Is it invading from an alternate dimension.
6. The entrance has a [[01KWN86X6NJZMP5ZESZTNDXY0P]] feel, where it feel like forces beyond your comprehension are breaking into the ordinary world.
7. Even though the old woman is very much a background character whom I tend to forget exists until that event, she still adds a lot of context where the scene would feel blander without her role in it. (the player is actually teleported to that location at a certain point, presumably because players had no way of knowing that they should return to that otherwise inconsequential location at such-and-such a point.)

I especially like the door closing on its own.  If this was a Steven King story it would feel almost normal, but here it is a surprising touch.  It feels like malevolent forces you cannot perceive are suddenly at work here.

## Pilgrim Butterflies - DS3

The Pilgrim Butterflies are treelike, floating monstrosities hovering in the background around Lothric castle.

The player doesn't interact with them other than one boss battle, where they occasionally spam you with ranged magic attacks.

While they don't do much, they add so much depth and atmosphere to the castle environs.

It adds even more impact in that they *sometimes* interact with the player.

There's a sort of tense ambiguity to their existence—most of the time they don't interfere with the player, but they are still opponents.  They are still dangers.

There's a lot of mystery surrounding them:

1. What are they doing there?
2. Why aren't they attacking me?
3. Will they suddenly start attacking me?
4. What would happen to me if the *did* attack me?
