# How to depict relying on Jesus?

# The root problem: Sacrilegious analogy

For over ten years I have wrestled with how to depict the Christian walk using fantasy metaphors.

The root problem with analogies to holy things is they associate holy attributes with objects that do not normally possess those attributes.

Almost by definition, analogies of holy things are mockeries.

# Call out

I guess I softened that a little in the first book by calling out the issue many times.

The Christ character referred to himself as a pale reflection.

Marloth was described as a “humble” book, which knew it was a fairy tale and that there is a world beyond itself.

# Reading the book

In the first book, the primary analogy for relying on Jesus was reading Marloth.

I feel like I stretched that analogy a little thin.

Part of the problem is I struggled with how to dive into detail about it.  I went out of my way to keep it vague and general

I think that mostly worked, but it was a bit of smoke and mirrors that I’m not sure I want to further extend it.

Also, I was able to get away with it a little more because it was a personal activity for James.  Now I need a more corporate activity everyone can engage in.

Also, while the intent is to make the story more immersive, the book within a book idea can easily become pretentious. 

# Obedience

Spiritual cultivation is focused on more in the New Testament.

The Old Testament emphasizes obedience.

There is a circular reinforcement where the Law is not arbitrary.  Following the law cultivates spiritual health, which in turn reinforces obedience.

# Profession

The New Testament places significant weight on profession and faith.

In an age when people were being killed for professing faith in Jesus Christ, profession meant a lot.  Christians need a healthy diet, but that is somewhat implicit when being martyred.

# Practice

> 🚫 For now I’m nixing this idea, not due to whether it is possible or not, but because I think I would like to focus more on spiritual sustenance.

Perhaps I can focus less on input practices, and focus more on output practices.  I don’t want to de-value input practices, but simply limit the scope.  At its core, Marloth is a battle of ideologies.  It sometimes delves into details but is primarily high level.

It may be impossible to do this in a healthy manner, in which case I’ll need to back up.  But it’s worth exploring first.

# Spiritual survival

On the flip side, I’ve been toying with survival horror for years, and nothing fits that better than spiritual survival.  I’ve often though that modern fiction writers have run too far with the idea of spiritual warfare, simply because it is one of the few analogies that can directly translate to more fantastic narratives.

The Christian life is much broader than spiritual warfare, I *am* fascinated by the idea of needing to struggle in a hostile world for crumbs of spiritual nourishment.

Really, that’s an idea I can see making core and building the bulk of the narrative around.

So for now I’ll nix the previous section on practice.

# Call out revisited

Perhaps instead of periodically injecting call-outs like I did in the first book, like some kind of fine-text-disclaimer, maybe I can more fundamentally break them into the ideology of Marloth’s spiritual analogies.

# Scarce and precious

Contrast is such a powerful tool.

Perhaps it will be easier to inject spiritual sustenance into the story if I make it scarce and precious.  People have to search and scrimp to find flickers of holiness to nourish their souls.

> 💡 That idea thrills me.  The story potential is so rich.
