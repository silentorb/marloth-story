# Recipe

In general, Marloth curses should consist of the following elements derived from the above Genesis passage:

1. Blessings
2. Law
3. Temptation
4. Evil empowerment
5. Negative consequences
6. A means of redemption

Along the elements derived from the Bible, I want curses to also include the following features:

1. Supernatural mechanics
2. A theme
3. Horror

# Blessing

Sin should not be framed as a contrast between good and neutral.

The context of sin is good and evil.

Every law God bestows is accompanied by blessings.

Rejecting God requires rejection of blessings.

It is natural for the world to frame temptation and sin in isolation, independent of the good things God has bestowed on the sinner.

It is like a rebellious child who has no understanding how dependent he is upon his parents.

> 💡 So much of sin and temptation are rooted in discontent.

# Law

In general, sin requires a law to break.

Law and law-breaking provide essential structure to sin and its consequences.

As with blessings, it is natural for the world to ignore the law when depicting temptation and sin.

Some sense of sin is expressed without any clear boundary as to what has been transgressed.

# Temptation

Temptation doesn’t always need to be depicted for each curse, but it should be at least implicitly in the mix.

Many temptations can include a tempter, though a tempter is not essential.

# Evil empowerment

To a small degree, all cursed characters in Marloth should be  https://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Main/CursedWithAwesome.

In particular, cursed with supernatural power.

The main reason is so each curse-bearer can be a villainous threat.

However, I want to emphasize that it should only be to a small degree.

The curse-bearers should only be given a slight Earthly edge.

I don’t want any of them to have god-like powers.

# Negative consequences

The curse-bearers cannot simply be https://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Main/CursedWithAwesome—the curse still needs to be a *curse*.

# A means of redemption

Every curse should have a remedy.

The remedy is not some malicious, arbitrary act like in some fairy tales.

The remedy involves repentance, submitting to God, and turning from sin.

Most cursed characters reject the remedy.

> For although they knew God, they neither glorified him as God nor gave thanks to him, but their thinking became futile and their foolish hearts were darkened. - Romans 1:21

> But because of your stubbornness and your unrepentant heart, you are storing up wrath against yourself for the day of God’s wrath, when his righteous judgment will be revealed. - Romans 2:5

> 💡 Not every remedy needs to be explicitly communicated in the story.
> This just needs to be a general theme, where after a while the audience can rightly assume that every curse must have the remedy.

# Corruption

Some magic that turns people into monsters.

Most of the denizens are already wicked, they would just become more wicked and mindless.

In a way this would be like a zombie or vampire epidemic, though I would use some other kinds of monstrosities.

> 💡 [[01KWN86X6NJZMP5ZESZTNDXY6D]] technically had a zombie storyline, except it was an abrupt transition and from that point on there was only one character left who could be zombified, and that character was never particularly worried about such a fate.

# Ghost stories

In horror stories, malevolent ghosts usually have one of two backstories. The ghost is either:

1. A victim who is now an avenging spirit (usually indiscriminately)
2. A victimizer who comes back from the dead to continue victimizing
