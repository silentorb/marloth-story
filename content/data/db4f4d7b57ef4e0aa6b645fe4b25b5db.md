> What you're describing is a fairly unusual narrative mode because most fiction treats space as a backdrop, while games often treat space as a primary system. You're looking for stories where:
>
> 1. The geometry of the environment is fully or largely knowable.
> 2. The audience can mentally map it.
> 3. Events depend on the actual spatial relationships.
> 4. The setting isn't merely a location but almost a character or machine.
> 5. The scale remains small enough that the entire space can be comprehended.
>
> *Dark Souls* is an interesting example because players often remember it less as a sequence of plot events and more as a giant interconnected spatial object. Discovering that two distant areas are actually adjacent is itself a narrative revelation.
>
> The closest term I know is **architectural storytelling**, but that usually refers to environmental storytelling and doesn't fully capture what you're after.
>
> A few related concepts:
>
> ### Topological Narrative
>
> The story is fundamentally about how spaces connect.
>
> The revelation isn't "who killed whom" but "how are these places related?"
>
> Examples include:
>
> * Sapphire & Steel
> * House of Leaves
> * Piranesi
>
> In these works, understanding the structure of the environment is inseparable from understanding the story.
>
> ### Closed-Space Narrative
>
> The environment forms a bounded system whose complete structure matters.
>
> Examples:
>
> * Cube
> * The Platform
> * The Tombs of Atuan
>
> The audience gradually learns the architecture, and that architecture drives events.
>
> ### Spatial Mystery
>
> The mystery concerns the world itself.
>
> Classic detective stories ask:
>
> > What happened?
>
> Spatial mysteries ask:
>
> > Where are we?
>
> or
>
> > How is this place constructed?
>
> Again, *Dark Souls* often operates this way.
>
> ***
>
> What I find particularly interesting is your emphasis on **limited scale**.
>
> Many stories feature elaborate worlds, but very few allow the audience to hold the entire geometry in their head.
>
> There seems to be a threshold beyond which spatial understanding collapses:
>
> * A house can be understood.
> * A mansion can sometimes be understood.
> * A castle is difficult.
> * A continent is impossible.
>
> Once the audience can no longer mentally map the whole thing, geometry stops being a narrative tool and becomes scenery.
>
> This is why your *Sapphire & Steel* example is so good. Much of that show occurs in houses, apartments, railway stations, and office buildings. The audience can understand every corridor and room. When something impossible happens, it's meaningful because we know exactly where it happened relative to everything else.

> If I were trying to name the specific thing you're describing, I'd probably call it **geometric narrative** or **architectural narrative**:
>
> > A story in which the precise geometry and connectivity of a comprehensible physical space are essential to understanding both the events and meaning of the story.
>
> That's narrower than environmental storytelling and closer to what *Dark Souls* and *Sapphire & Steel* are doing. The environment isn't merely where the story occurs—it is one of the primary mechanisms by which the story is told.

> That's an important distinction, and I think it gets closer to what you're actually interested in.
>
> Many "architecture mystery" stories—like House of Leaves or The Strange House—make the geometry the **object of investigation**. The characters stand outside the structure, intellectually dissecting it.
>
> What you're describing sounds more like geometry as **lived reality**.
>
> The characters inhabit the space and make decisions within it. The audience learns the geometry not because someone is drawing floor plans, but because the unfolding events force everyone to understand the space.
>
> That's much closer to how games work.
>
> In *Dark Souls*, nobody sits down and explains the map. Instead:
>
> * You discover shortcuts.
>
> * You learn where enemies patrol.
>
> * You understand lines of sight.
>
> * You recognize vertical relationships.
>
> * You realize two places are adjacent.
>
> The story emerges from inhabiting the geometry.
>
> ***
>
> One way to think about it is that there are two different modes:
>
> | Geometry as Puzzle        | Geometry as Habitat                 |
> | :------------------------ | :---------------------------------- |
> | "What is this place?"     | "How do people live in this place?" |
> | Analysis-driven           | Experience-driven                   |
> | Floor plans are clues     | Floor plans are reality             |
> | The building is a mystery | The building is a world             |
>
> *The Strange House* is mostly on the left side.
>
> *Sapphire & Steel* often drifts toward the right side. The characters move through rooms whose arrangement affects events, and the audience gradually internalizes the layout because it matters operationally.
>
> ***
>
> This also explains why games are unusually good at this.
>
> Most novels and films can get away with vague geography:
>
> > They ran through the castle.
>
> That's sufficient.
>
> Games can't.
>
> The player must know:
>
> * Which staircase?
>
> * Which corridor?
>
> * How many rooms away?
>
> * What overlooks what?
>
> As a result, games accidentally developed narrative techniques that treat space as something concrete.
>
> ***
>
> Another aspect that I think is central to your interest is **finite comprehensibility**.
>
> A lot of fantasy worlds are large but not geometrically meaningful.
>
> You can draw a map of The Lord of the Rings, but the exact shape of a hallway in Minas Tirith rarely matters.
>
> By contrast, in a well-designed *Dark Souls* area, changing a staircase location might completely alter the narrative experience.
>
> The geometry is operating at a human scale where every connection matters.
>
> ***
>
> This makes me think of architecture almost functioning as a dramatic system.
>
> Imagine a setting consisting of:
>
> * twelve rooms,
>
> * three staircases,
>
> * two hidden passages,
>
> * one roof.
>
> If every character's movement is constrained by that layout, then the architecture itself begins generating story possibilities.
>
> The geometry creates:
>
> * encounters,
>
> * separations,
>
> * escapes,
>
> * discoveries,
>
> * misunderstandings,
>
> * ambushes,
>
> * moments of revelation.
>
> In that sense the building becomes something closer to a game board than a backdrop.
>
> ***
>
> What's especially rare is finding non-game media that trusts the audience to build and retain a mental map.
>
> Most film and television deliberately avoid requiring that level of spatial reasoning.
>
> Games train players to think:
>
> > "If I go up one level and cross the bridge, I'll emerge above the room I visited earlier."
>
> Most passive media instead train audiences to think:
>
> > "The characters have moved somewhere else."
>
> That's one reason works like *Sapphire & Steel* feel unusual. They occasionally ask the audience to engage with space the way a player engages with a game level.
>
> I suspect that what attracts you isn't merely "architecture matters," but a stronger idea:
>
> > The story is partially encoded in the physical arrangement of a finite, fully comprehensible space.
>
> The audience isn't just watching events happen in the setting. They're gradually learning the setting itself, and that growing spatial understanding becomes part of understanding the story. That's a very game-like form of narrative, even when it appears in television, novels, or manga.
