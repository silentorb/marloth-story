# Extracting dark forest qualities from The Witcher

The Witcher series of media is the best example I’ve seen of how I want to implement a [[181a3aae0f4b4056b6c28bb49e27978e]] setting.

I’ve seen many other examples of this idea but they have always felt incomplete and like teases.  The Witcher feels fully fleshed out in the way it executes this vision I am interested in.

> 💡 Note that one difference between what I want to do with a Marloth [[181a3aae0f4b4056b6c28bb49e27978e]] and the Witcher is I want the Marloth Dark Forest to be more dreamlike and mysterious.
> It’s possible that desire may break a little of the Witcher recipe and how it succeeds, but I want to at least experiment first and see what happens.

> 💡 This relates to [[b9b9e0b8df614e64bae9bf2ec5b048a2]].

# Witcher dark forest attributes

It would be helpful to enumerate the qualities of the Witcher that make it effective at a fully fleshed out dark forest setting, and differentiates it from the other examples I’ve found.

| Witcher | Others | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Balanced mix of village and forest | Some dark forest settings don’t have enough *village*, or they revolve around a village and the surrounding forest isn’t really fleshed out and feels like background (like an island surrounded by ocean). |  |
| Wide variety of monsters | Most horror stories in a forest only have a single monster, or multiple monsters of the same type.

When traveling between locations, there is usually a single danger the character needs to be careful about, which makes the forest itself feel relatively safe.  (I want the forest *itself* to feel dangerous.) |  |
| Rich monster lore | So often monsters have little significance beyond a danger to be feared. |  |
| Rich monster technicalities | So often the mechanics of the monster are shallow and causality is spontaneously contrived by the author. |  |
| The natural science of the forest plays into the narrative. | Not much nature.  Nature is mostly a backdrop. | I’m focusing on monsters in these points, but for this entry I think having some non-monster, fantastic natural threats (like poisonous bogs) would help bring out the natural forest elements. |
| People are aware of the monsters, and to some degree no how to coexist with them. | Usually either most of the characters don’t believe in monsters, or its only locals that believe in monsters while outsiders don’t. |  |
| Monsters can be slain, though it requires expertise. | Often the monsters are nearly impossible to kill, even by police, military, and monster hunters. |  |
| The protagonist is a monster hunter. | Usually the main characters know little or nothing about the monsters and how to fight them. | The audience is given a direct connection to the topic of monsters via the main character. |
| Geography and maps play a key part in the narrative.

Travel between well defined landmarks is a key part of the narrative. | Usually geography and landmarks are vague.  The audience is provided no sense of spatial relationship. | In some respects this is a precedent established by Tolkien. |

# Dreamifying the recipe

At a glance, most of those ingredients do not conflict with a more dreamlike recipe, except for the geography ingredient.

Independent of this immediate context, I feel like that is a larger tension I have been wrestling with for a while and is probably worth a dedicated article.
